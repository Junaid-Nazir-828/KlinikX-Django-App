PRK - Surface Ablation for Vision Correction

PRK (Photorefractive Keratectomy) is a surface ablation laser eye surgery used to correct myopia, hyperopia, and astigmatism. The procedure involves removing the outer layer of the cornea (epithelium) and reshaping the cornea using an excimer laser to improve vision.

Photorefractive Keratectomy (PRK) is a refractive eye surgery procedure designed to correct vision problems such as nearsightedness, farsightedness, and astigmatism. It is considered one of the earliest forms of laser vision correction and has been used successfully for several decades.

PRK Procedure

  • Removal of the corneal epithelium.
  • Reshaping of the cornea using an excimer laser.
  • No corneal flap creation.
  • Suitable for patients with thinner corneas.

How PRK Works

During PRK, the surgeon uses a laser to remove a thin layer of the cornea's surface epithelial cells. This allows the underlying cornea to be reshaped, which corrects the refractive errors causing vision problems. Unlike LASIK, PRK does not involve creating a corneal flap; instead, the protective epithelium is temporarily removed, promoting natural healing after the procedure.

Advantages of PRK

  • Less risk of flap-related complications.
  • Preserves more corneal tissue.
  • Suitable for patients with thin corneas or larger pupils.
  • Low risk of dry eye after healing.

Candidate Selection

Candidates for PRK must have stable vision prescriptions and be in good overall eye health. Ideal candidates should not have certain conditions, such as severe dry eye or thin corneas, which may disqualify them from the procedure. A comprehensive eye examination and consultation with an eye surgeon are essential to determine a patient's eligibility.

Recovery and Healing Process

After PRK, patients may experience mild discomfort and sensitivity to light for a few days. The cornea's surface epithelial cells regenerate over time, leading to improved vision. It may take several weeks for vision to stabilize fully. During the recovery period, patients must follow their surgeon's instructions carefully and attend follow-up appointments.

Benefits and Advantages of PRK

PRK offers several advantages, such as no corneal flap creation, making it a suitable option for individuals with thin corneas or other corneal issues. The procedure is also less invasive than LASIK, with a reduced risk of certain complications associated with flap creation. Additionally, PRK can be an excellent alternative for those not eligible for LASIK.

Potential Risks and Side Effects

As with any surgical procedure, PRK carries some risks. These may include temporary light sensitivity, dry eyes, halos, glare, and mild discomfort during the healing phase. In rare cases, more severe complications, such as infection or corneal haze, may occur, but they are infrequent and usually treatable.

Comparing PRK to Other Vision Correction Procedures

PRK, LASIK, and other vision correction procedures have unique benefits and considerations. While PRK lacks the immediate visual recovery of LASIK due to the absence of a corneal flap, it may be preferred for certain patients based on their individual eye characteristics and health.
